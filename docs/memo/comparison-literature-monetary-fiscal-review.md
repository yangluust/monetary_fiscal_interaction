## 1. Misalignment Log

## Bassetto and Miller (2025), `A Monetary-Fiscal Theory of Sudden Inflations`

- Memo statement: the economy features a "representative household" that decides whether to acquire costly information about future fiscal surpluses.
- Why not aligned: in the richer information-acquisition setup, the costly-information decision is attached to price setters in a Ma\'ckowiak-Wiederholt style environment, while shoppers infer from prices. The model is not best described as a single representative household choosing attention.
- Tighter correction: describe the framework as having a continuum of agents with price setters who may acquire costly information, rather than a representative household that directly chooses attention.

- Memo statement: the model has a finite three-period structure in which agents acquire information in period 2 and fiscal outcomes are realized in period 3.
- Why not aligned: the paper does use a three-period long-run structure, but the memo overstates the interpretation by making it sound like a literal household dynamic optimization problem. The key three-period structure is about fiscal backing, price-level determination, and information sensitivity of long-run inflation.
- Tighter correction: say the paper uses a stylized three-period structure to study sudden inflations and long-run price-level determination under different monetary-fiscal regimes.

- Memo statement: monetary policy is "deliberately simplified" and plays only a limited operational role.
- Why not aligned: monetary policy is explicit. The baseline uses a fixed nominal interest rate peg, and the paper also discusses how alternative policy rules would affect propagation.
- Tighter correction: say monetary policy is modeled parsimoniously through an interest-rate peg rather than omitted.

- Memo statement: inflation is determined primarily by expectations about future fiscal backing rather than by explicit monetary policy instruments.
- Why not aligned: this is too one-sided. A central feature of the paper is the interaction between fiscal backing and the monetary regime. In monetary-led states the fiscal authority adjusts to validate a price target, while in fiscally led states inflation absorbs fiscal stress.
- Tighter correction: say inflation depends on the interaction between fiscal backing and the monetary-fiscal regime, with fiscal stress mattering most in fiscally led states.

- Memo statement: fiscal policy operates only through expectations about future surpluses and debt is inherited rather than actively chosen.
- Why not aligned: the paper is not a strategic debt-issuance model, but the memo makes the fiscal side sound entirely passive. The paper's core distinction is whether taxes adjust to support a price target or whether inflation adjusts to restore government solvency.
- Tighter correction: emphasize regime-dependent fiscal backing rather than a blanket statement that fiscal policy is passive.

- Memo statement: the mechanism relies on "complementarities in information acquisition" that can produce multiple equilibria.
- Why not aligned: this is directionally right, but the paper is more precise: the multiplicity comes from equilibrium regions in which both high- and low-information outcomes can be supported.
- Tighter correction: say the paper can feature multiple information-acquisition equilibria over some regions, rather than presenting multiplicity as the generic mechanism.

## Arellano, Bai, and Mihalache (2026), `Monetary Policy and Sovereign Risk in Emerging Economies (NK-Default)`

- Memo statement: the government issues nominal debt subject to default risk.
- Why not aligned: in the baseline NK-Default model, sovereign borrowing is long-term debt denominated in foreign currency and held by international lenders.
- Tighter correction: say the government borrows externally through long-term foreign-currency debt, with local nominal interest rates set by the monetary authority.

- Memo statement: households "hold government bonds."
- Why not aligned: the defaultable sovereign debt is external and held by foreign lenders. Domestic bonds appear in the household problem but clear in zero net supply.
- Tighter correction: distinguish clearly between domestic nominal bonds in zero net supply and the government's external defaultable debt held abroad.

- Memo statement: tighter monetary policy raises sovereign spreads and default risk by increasing borrowing costs.
- Why not aligned: this reverses one of the paper's main mechanisms. The paper stresses monetary discipline: tighter monetary policy discourages overborrowing and can lower equilibrium spreads and default risk.
- Tighter correction: say default risk can raise inflation through expectations, while tighter monetary policy can reduce borrowing incentives and bring spreads down.

- Memo statement: accommodative monetary policy reduces borrowing costs and default risk, though it may raise inflation.
- Why not aligned: the paper does not present easing as uniformly reducing default risk. Looser policy weakens the disciplining effect on sovereign borrowing.
- Tighter correction: say easing may relieve financing conditions in partial equilibrium, but in the model it can worsen borrowing incentives and raise default risk.

- Memo statement: fiscal policy is driven primarily by financing needs rather than strategic considerations.
- Why not aligned: there is no signaling, but borrowing is still an optimal state-contingent choice inside a dynamic sovereign default problem.
- Tighter correction: say the government optimally chooses borrowing and default subject to bond pricing and default amplification, rather than characterizing borrowing as merely mechanical financing.

## Bassetto and Galli (2019), `Is Inflation Default? The Role of Information in Debt Crises`

- Memo statement: the model features infinite-horizon heterogeneous investors.
- Why not aligned: the core model is a three-period environment.
- Tighter correction: say the paper uses a finite three-period setup to compare inflation and default under dispersed information.

- Memo statement: there are informed and uninformed investors in a simple binary sense.
- Why not aligned: the paper's information structure is more precise: agents receive dispersed private signals, and the key distinction is often which agents are marginal in pricing debt at different stages.
- Tighter correction: describe the model as one with dispersed private information and heterogeneous signal precision, rather than a simple informed-versus-uninformed split.

- Memo statement: the government issues both domestic-currency nominal debt and foreign-currency debt, and the debt composition is central.
- Why not aligned: the paper's comparison is framed as Euro-versus-Yen type environments with matched payoff losses, not as a single optimal debt-composition problem.
- Tighter correction: say the paper compares environments where crisis resolution occurs through inflation or outright default, holding the payoff loss comparable, to isolate the role of information.

- Memo statement: fiscal policy determines borrowing and repayment decisions in a rich intertemporal optimization problem.
- Why not aligned: the fiscal structure is much more mechanical than the memo suggests. The paper is designed to isolate information and pricing rather than solve a rich fiscal policy problem.
- Tighter correction: describe the debt and repayment structure as a stylized environment used to study how information changes the relative sensitivity of inflationary and default outcomes.

- Memo statement: crises arise because informed investors revise beliefs first and reduce debt demand, producing sudden inflation or default.
- Why not aligned: this misses the paper's sharper point. The important comparison is how the information held by marginal traders affects price sensitivity in inflationary versus default-type environments.
- Tighter correction: emphasize higher-order beliefs, marginal traders, and the differential responsiveness of prices to news across inflationary and default settings.

## Bianchi and Ilut (2017), `Monetary/Fiscal Policy Mix and Agents' Beliefs`

- Memo statement: the paper studies how regime uncertainty affects sovereign risk and sovereign spreads.
- Why not aligned: the paper is about the US Great Inflation, debt dynamics, long-bond prices, and the interaction of monetary and fiscal regimes. It is not a sovereign default or emerging-market spread paper.
- Tighter correction: say the paper studies inflation, debt dynamics, long-bond valuation, and the role of beliefs about the monetary-fiscal policy mix.

- Memo statement: the economy switches between monetary-dominance and fiscal-dominance regimes.
- Why not aligned: the paper has three policy regimes, not two: `AM/PF`, `PM/AF`, and `AM/AF`.
- Tighter correction: refer explicitly to three regimes, including the conflict regime in which both authorities are active.

- Memo statement: agents observe outcomes and infer transition probabilities through a learning process.
- Why not aligned: the paper is not a private-learning model in that sense. Agents are rational and know the Markov-switching structure; the beliefs channel operates through expectations over regime changes, not through asymmetric information.
- Tighter correction: say agents form expectations over recurrent regime changes under a known Markov structure.

- Memo statement: higher perceived fiscal dominance raises sovereign spreads and borrowing costs.
- Why not aligned: the relevant asset-pricing objects are inflation, real rates, and long-bond prices, not sovereign default spreads.
- Tighter correction: say beliefs about fiscal backing alter inflation expectations, real rates, and long-term bond valuations.

- Memo statement: government bonds are treated too generically.
- Why not aligned: the paper's maturity structure matters for the transmission mechanism, especially through long-bond prices.
- Tighter correction: mention that long-term bond prices are part of the core mechanism.

## Bianchi and Mondragon (2022), `Monetary Independence and Rollover Crises`

- Memo statement: the paper emphasizes future monetary accommodation of fiscal imbalances and inflation dynamics.
- Why not aligned: the baseline mechanism is not debt dilution through inflation. It is about how the loss of monetary independence worsens recessions during market shutoffs, making rollover crises more likely.
- Tighter correction: lead with monetary independence as a macro-stabilization tool under nominal rigidities, not inflationary accommodation of debt.

- Memo statement: the primary asset is short-term sovereign debt.
- Why not aligned: the model uses long-term sovereign bonds.
- Tighter correction: say the government issues long-term foreign-currency debt in a sovereign default model with rollover crises.

- Memo statement: investors worry that the central bank may accommodate fiscal pressures through inflation.
- Why not aligned: in the baseline, the debt is foreign-currency or real debt, so the government cannot inflate away its value.
- Tighter correction: say the key issue is whether monetary independence can stabilize demand and employment during a funding crisis, not whether inflation can erode the debt burden.

- Memo statement: the model's mechanism runs from higher borrowing costs to inflationary accommodation.
- Why not aligned: the mechanism instead runs through a panic-driven fiscal contraction, aggregate-demand collapse, unemployment under nominal rigidities, and a higher incentive to default.
- Tighter correction: emphasize recession amplification and self-fulfilling rollover crises.

- Memo statement: the model features homogeneous investors who uniformly price government debt.
- Why not aligned: this hides the distinction between domestic households and foreign lenders.
- Tighter correction: distinguish domestic agents from foreign lenders rather than collapsing the environment into a homogeneous-investor description.

## 2. New Paper Summaries

## Espino, Kozlowski, Martin, and Sanchez (2025), `Domestic Policies and Sovereign Default`

### Agents

The paper studies a small open economy with representative infinitely lived households, firms producing tradable and nontradable goods, a government that chooses fiscal and monetary policy, and foreign risk-neutral lenders. The government's problem includes borrowing, taxation, money creation, and default.

### Time Horizon and Timing

The environment is infinite-horizon and recursive in discrete time. Each period the government chooses policies without commitment to future debt repayment or future policy actions, and aggregate shocks such as terms-of-trade or productivity shocks affect borrowing conditions and macro outcomes.

### Assets and Who Issues Them

The government issues external sovereign debt in foreign currency to foreign investors. Domestic government liabilities take the form of fiat money used for transactions. This distinction matters: inflation does not primarily work by eroding a stock of domestic nominal sovereign bonds, but through money creation, exchange-rate depreciation, and domestic distortions.

### Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is implemented through money growth in a cash-in-advance style environment for nontradable consumption. The paper studies when monetary policy is actively used to support fiscal policy and shows that distortionary inflation arises when the government lacks lump-sum financing and cannot commit. This is not a central-bank-balance-sheet model with reserves, interest on reserves, or open-market operations.

### Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy uses distortionary labor taxes, public expenditure, transfers, and external borrowing. A generalized Euler equation characterizes debt choice. The debt decision reflects distortion smoothing, default premia, and the interaction between future distortions and current money demand.

### Informational Asymmetries

The paper does not rely on asymmetric information, private types, or signaling. The main friction is lack of commitment, not hidden information.

### Economic Mechanism

Adverse shocks raise the cost of rolling over external debt. In response, the government reduces debt, raises taxes, lowers spending, increases money creation, and allows faster currency depreciation. The paper's central contribution is to explain the joint movement of sovereign spreads, inflation, currency depreciation, and procyclical fiscal policy in emerging markets when distortionary domestic policies interact with sovereign default risk.

### Contrast with your framework

- The paper does not model a separate central bank balance sheet with reserves or remittances.
- Sovereign debt is foreign-currency debt, not an endogenous stock of domestic nominal debt used for signaling.
- Default is central, but signaling is absent.
- Monetary-fiscal interaction works through distortionary taxation, seigniorage, and rollover costs rather than through issuance as a signal under asymmetric information.

## Hurtado, Nuno, and Thomas (2023), `Monetary Policy and Sovereign Debt Sustainability`

### Agents

The paper studies a small open economy with risk-averse domestic households, a benevolent government, and foreign investors who price sovereign bonds. The government chooses fiscal and monetary policy under discretion and can default outright.

### Time Horizon and Timing

The framework is continuous time and infinite horizon. The government repeatedly chooses inflation, borrowing, and default under no commitment, and the debt-income state determines how close the economy is to the default frontier.

### Assets and Who Issues Them

The government issues long-term nominal bonds denominated in domestic currency to foreign investors. Because the debt is nominal, inflation can erode its real value. This is crucial to the paper's comparison between outright default and inflation as a partial default instrument.

### Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is the discretionary choice of inflation, subject to welfare costs. The paper does not model a central bank balance sheet, reserves, or an interest-rate rule. Instead, it studies the trade-off between using inflation to stabilize debt and the inflationary bias created by discretion.

### Fiscal Policy

Fiscal and monetary choices are jointly determined under discretion. The government chooses borrowing and can default, while inflation changes the repayment region by reducing the real burden of nominal debt. The fiscal side is therefore endogenous, but the paper is not about signaling through issuance.

### Informational Asymmetries

The paper assumes full information. There are no private types, noisy signals, or asymmetric-information mechanisms.

### Economic Mechanism

Inflation acts as a state-contingent partial default that can substitute for outright default. This enlarges the repayment region and can raise welfare near the default frontier because inflation is a more flexible tool than outright default. Over the long run, however, the welfare gains are modest because inflationary bias remains costly when debt stress is low.

### Contrast with your framework

- The paper has no explicit central bank balance sheet or reserve-remittance channel.
- Endogenous nominal debt and inflation are central, but signaling is absent.
- Default and inflation are jointly determined, with inflation functioning as partial default.
- The main friction is discretion and inflationary bias, not asymmetric information.
